⚡ Case Study 01

Listing Doctor

Designing a zero-to-one application to track, diagnose, and resolve product listing errors across Amazon, Walmart, and eBay marketplaces.

UX Research Product Design Design System Usability Testing E-commerce
My Role
Lead UX Designer
Timeline
5 Months · 2023
Team
2 Designers · 4 Eng
Platform
Web App · SaaS
The Problem

Sellers on multi-channel marketplaces lose significant revenue due to undetected listing errors — suppressed products, missing attributes, policy violations — that are impossible to monitor manually across thousands of SKUs.

The Solution

A centralized dashboard that aggregates listing health data from Amazon, Walmart, and eBay APIs, surfaces critical errors with severity scoring, and guides sellers through step-by-step resolution workflows.

01

Discovery & Research発見

I conducted 12 contextual inquiry sessions with e-commerce operations managers and sellers. The goal was to understand the current workaround landscape — what tools they used, where errors fell through the cracks, and what the emotional cost of a suppressed listing felt like.

😰
Manual monitoring is the norm
9 of 12 participants used spreadsheets or marketplace-native dashboards in separate tabs to monitor listing health — no unified view existed.
⏱️
Detection lag averages 4.2 days
On average, sellers didn't notice a suppressed listing for 4+ days — each day representing direct lost revenue on that SKU.
🤯
Error messages are cryptic
Marketplace error codes are written for developers, not merchants. "8572: INVALID_ATTRIBUTE_VALUE" means nothing without a glossary.
🔁
Repeat errors are preventable
68% of errors seen were recurring — sellers were solving the same issues over and over due to no historical error memory in existing tools.
"I found out my top-selling product was suppressed on Amazon because I noticed a dip in sales — not because any tool told me."
— Operations Manager, Mid-market Seller · Research Participant
02

Design Process過程

The design went through 4 major iterations, each validated with a different cohort of sellers. I used a mix of low-fidelity concept testing, task-based usability testing, and desirability studies to pressure-test each direction.

🗺️
Journey Mapping & How Might We
Mapped the current-state error discovery journey from suppression event → seller awareness → resolution. Identified 6 key failure points. Generated 40+ HMW statements, narrowed to 8 priority opportunity areas.
✏️
Concept Sketching & Information Architecture
Sketched 3 competing IA approaches: a flat list view, a marketplace-grouped view, and a severity-first priority view. Tested concept preference with 6 participants — severity-first won decisively.
🖥️
Wireframe Usability Testing (Round 1 & 2)
Built mid-fi wireframes in Figma, ran 2 rounds of task-based usability tests with 5 participants each. Success rates moved from 62% → 89% on core task flows between rounds.
🎨
High-Fidelity Design & Design System
Built a component library of 80+ components in Figma with error state variants, severity indicators, and multi-marketplace theming. Developed motion principles for real-time error updates.
03

Key Design Decisions決断

app.listingdoctor.com/dashboard
DASHBOARD WIREFRAME · SEVERITY-FIRST LAYOUT
Errors listed chronologically — latest errors first, regardless of severity. Merchants missed critical suppression events buried under minor warnings.
Severity-first triage queue. Critical errors (suppressed listings) always surface at the top, with a 3-tier system: Critical / Warning / Info. Reduced time-to-action on suppressed SKUs by ~70%.
Raw API error codes displayed (e.g. "ERROR_8572_INVALID_ATTRIBUTE"). Required sellers to Google every error code to understand it.
Human-readable error translation layer. "Your product title exceeds Amazon's 200-character limit. Current: 214 chars. Here's how to fix it." Inline with a one-click edit shortcut.
04

Outcomes結果

89% Task Completion Rate
4.2d→
6hr Detection Lag Reduced
4.7/5 SUS Usability Score
✓
Zero-to-one shipped — product went from concept to live beta with 40 merchant accounts in 5 months.
✓
Design system adopted — 80+ component library now used across 3 products in the broader platform ecosystem.
✓
Reduced support tickets by 34% in the first 6 weeks post-launch due to in-context guidance and error translation.
05

Reflection反省

The biggest lesson from Listing Doctor was the power of domain immersion. E-commerce operations is a deeply specialized world — spending the first 3 weeks purely in discovery before touching Figma made all the difference. The severity-first design wouldn't have emerged without understanding the real emotional weight of a suppressed listing on a small business owner.

If I were to revisit, I'd invest more time in notification design — getting sellers to act on alerts outside the app, not just inside it, was a UX challenge we addressed in v2 but could have been baked into the original brief.

🔬 Case Study 02

SaaS Contract Analyzer

Augmenting the financial auditing process by designing human-centered interfaces for ML-powered contract analysis and anomaly detection.

AI/ML UX B2B SaaS Fintech Information Architecture Prototyping
My Role
Senior UX Designer
Timeline
4 Months · 2023
Team
1 Designer · ML Team · 3 Eng
Platform
Web App · Enterprise
The Challenge

Financial auditors review hundreds of SaaS contracts manually — checking for auto-renewal clauses, price escalators, and compliance flags. The ML model could identify these patterns, but the outputs were impenetrable for non-technical auditors.

My Contribution

Bridge the gap between ML confidence scores and human judgment. Design an interface where auditors could review, override, and learn from model outputs — turning AI into a trusted collaborator, not a black box.

01

The AI Trust Problem信頼

The core UX challenge here wasn't a flow or navigation problem — it was a trust calibration problem. Auditors were either over-trusting the model (rubber-stamping outputs) or completely ignoring it (doing everything manually anyway). Neither extreme was useful.

"When the model flags something at 72% confidence, what does that even mean? Do I trust it or not? I need to understand *why* it thinks that."
— Senior Financial Auditor · Research Participant
🧠
Explainability is non-negotiable
Auditors needed to see the specific contract clause that triggered a flag — not just a confidence score. Evidence-first design became the core principle.
✍️
Override ≠ failure
Auditors needed a frictionless way to override ML flags without feeling like they were "breaking" something. Override flows needed to capture reasoning to improve the model over time.
📄
Side-by-side context is critical
Auditors could not trust a flag without seeing the exact contract text that triggered it. The document viewer and analysis panel had to be co-visible at all times.
⚡
Batch workflows matter
Power users processed 20+ contracts per session. Keyboard shortcuts, bulk approval, and "similar contracts" grouping were critical for efficiency at scale.
02

Design Decisions設計

Confidence score shown as a raw percentage (e.g. 78%). No context about what this means or what evidence drove it.
Evidence-anchored flags. Every flag highlights the exact contract clause, with the specific language that triggered it. Confidence shown as a 3-tier label (High / Medium / Review) tied to clear thresholds.
Overriding a model flag required navigating to a separate "disputes" section, adding friction that discouraged auditors from correcting wrong outputs.
Inline override with reasoning capture. A single keystroke (or click) surfaces an override panel with reason codes. This data feeds directly back into model retraining — turning disagreement into improvement.
03

Outcomes成果

3.4× Faster Contract Review
91% Auditor Trust Score (Post)
↑12% Model Accuracy (Override Loop)

🏛️ Case Study 03

Smithsonian Archives of American Art

Reimagining search and discovery for one of the world's largest archives of visual art documentation — serving scholars, researchers, and the curious public.

Information Architecture Search UX Accessibility Cultural UX Research-led
My Role
UX Designer (Lead)
Timeline
6 Months · 2022
Team
2 Designers · Archivists
Platform
Web · Public Institution
Context

The Smithsonian's Archives of American Art holds over 20 million items — letters, sketchbooks, photos, oral histories — documenting American art history. The existing search experience made this treasure largely undiscoverable.

My Role

Lead the end-to-end redesign of the search and browse experience. Balance the needs of academic researchers who need precision with casual visitors who want inspiration and serendipitous discovery.

01

The Dual User Challenge二重

The most complex challenge here was designing for two radically different mental models on the same interface. A PhD art historian searching by provenance, date range, and medium has completely different needs than a high school student browsing for a Frida Kahlo reference photo.

🎓
Researchers need precision tools
Faceted search with 12+ filter dimensions, Boolean operators, date range sliders, and the ability to save and share search queries. Depth over breadth.
🌿
General visitors need serendipity
Visual browse modes, "Related works" connections, artist timelines, and thematic collections. Discovery over search. The archive as a museum, not a database.
♿
Accessibility is an ethical imperative
Public cultural institutions have a WCAG AA obligation. Every design decision was pressure-tested against screen reader compatibility, contrast ratios, and keyboard navigation.
📜
Metadata quality is uneven
Some collection items have rich metadata; others are a scan with a two-word description. The UI needed to gracefully handle both extremes without feeling broken.
02

Key Design Solution解決

We introduced a "Search Mode" toggle — a subtle but powerful interaction that transformed the interface from an academic search interface to a visual browse experience. Rather than creating two separate sites, the toggle revealed and hid complexity based on intent.

Single search experience tried to serve both audiences. Advanced filters cluttered the casual browse experience; removing them frustrated researchers.
Progressive disclosure with mode-switching. Default "Explore" mode is visual and spacious. "Research" mode surfaces all 12 filter dimensions. Both modes share the same URL structure for linking and citation.
Items with sparse metadata showed as nearly-empty cards — broken-feeling, unappealing, appearing low-quality compared to rich items.
Metadata-adaptive card templates. Cards intelligently resize content areas based on available metadata. Sparse items get a more visual treatment; rich items show structured data grids.
03

Outcomes成果

+47% Search Success Rate
WCAG AA Accessibility Rating
2.8× Time on Archive (Avg)

✈️ Case Study 04

Wingman

Integrating a Generative AI chatbot into a travel companion iOS app — designing conversational UX that feels genuinely human, contextual, and delightful to use.

Conversational UI iOS Design Gen AI Mobile UX Prompt Design
My Role
Lead Product Designer
Timeline
4 Months · 2023
Team
2 Designers · iOS · AI Eng
Platform
iOS · Mobile
The Challenge

Wingman had a powerful AI travel recommendation engine. But the chat interface felt cold and robotic — like texting a FAQ bot. Users disengaged after 1-2 messages. The AI's capability was invisible behind a poor conversational experience.

The Goal

Design a conversational experience where the AI feels like a knowledgeable local friend — proactive, contextual, with personality — while staying within the guardrails of LLM capabilities and honest about its limitations.

01

Designing for Conversation会話

Conversational UI design is a discipline of its own — it lives at the intersection of UX writing, interaction design, and prompt engineering. I led discovery research with 15 frequent travellers across Vancouver, Toronto, and Delhi to understand how people naturally ask for travel advice.

🎙️
Voice & Personality Definition
Defined the AI's personality spectrum: knowledgeable but not condescending, enthusiastic but not exhausting, honest about uncertainty. Created a voice guide with 20+ do/don't example exchanges.
🗺️
Conversation Flow Mapping
Mapped 8 core conversation journeys (itinerary planning, restaurant discovery, transport help, emergency situations, etc.) and identified the 12 most common "dead end" conversation patterns that needed graceful recovery design.
💬
Prompt Scaffolding Design
Worked directly with the AI engineering team to design conversation-starter suggestions, contextual prompts, and response format guidelines — making the model's outputs visually parseable and actionable within the iOS interface.
02

Key Innovations革新

🎯
Contextual Conversation Starters
When a user opens the app at 7pm in Tokyo, the AI surfaces "Good evening! Looking for dinner recommendations nearby?" — not a blank chat input. Location + time context drives proactive suggestions.
🃏
Rich Response Cards
AI responses that include places, restaurants, or transit steps render as interactive cards — not walls of text. Tap to open in Maps, save to itinerary, or share with travel partners.
🤍
Honest Uncertainty Design
Designed a distinct visual treatment for when the AI hedges ("I'm not 100% certain about opening hours — I'd verify on Google"). Users trusted the AI *more* because it acknowledged its limits.
🔁
Trip Memory & Continuity
The AI remembers your trip context across sessions — "How was that ramen place I suggested yesterday?" feels magical and creates genuine emotional engagement with the product.
"I forgot I was talking to an AI. It felt like messaging a friend who happened to know everything about Tokyo."
— Beta Tester · UX Feedback Session · Vancouver
03

Outcomes成果

4.8× Chat Session Length
78% D7 Retention Rate
4.6★ App Store Rating
✓
Conversation depth increased 4.8× — users went from 1-2 message exchanges to sustained multi-turn conversations averaging 12 messages.
✓
AI personality framework adopted as the internal standard for all AI-integrated features across the product suite.
✓
Featured in App Store's "Travel Apps We Love" editorial section, citing the conversational experience as a differentiator.
04

Reflection反省

Wingman taught me that designing for AI is fundamentally designing for uncertainty. Every other design discipline has deterministic outputs — you design a button, you know what it does. With LLMs, you're designing for a probability distribution of responses.

The most important skill I developed here was prompt scaffolding — learning to shape AI behavior through the design of context, constraints, and conversation starters, not just the UI chrome around them. This is a design skill that barely existed three years ago and is now central to my practice.
